Overview

During this homework, we implemented all parts of geometric modeling, using triangles and various operations on said triangles to display a 3D object. The first section involved using de Casteljau’s algorithm to render Bezier curves, before extrapolating this algorithm to a 3D plane to make Bezier surfaces. The second section involved rendering triangles and their normals to account for more complex lighting, before building up to a subdivision method of an existing mesh using edge manipulation methods. The result of this homework was a modeler that could subdivide any 3D asset made up of vertices and edges. What we found interesting was how the process of subdivision, despite looking like a completely arbitrary operation from a glance, was actually a complicated, yet deliberate process to mathematically compute the new edges and vertices, as well as their new positions.

Part 6: Loop subdivision for mesh upsampling

Briefly explain how you implemented the loop subdivision and describe any interesting implementation / debugging tricks you have used.
We followed the recommended directions in the spec, which goes as follows: compute the new positions for every vertex in the original mesh using the average of its surrounding vertices and the new positions for every vertex associated with edges using weighted averages of the vertexes in the quad. Next, iterating and splitting through every edge in the original mesh and marking new edges created such that they can be flipped, before finally applying the new positions to every vertex and moving them.

Take some notes, as well as some screenshots, of your observations on how meshes behave after loop subdivision. What happens to sharp corners and edges? Can you reduce this effect by pre-splitting some edges?
Sharp corners get smoothed out to become rounder, as according to this cube after its first subdivision. Edges also get rounded out as they get split and its surrounding vertices moved according to the loop subdivision rules, which reduces the length of each corresponding edge. This effect can be reduced by pre-splitting edges, as this exempts the edges from loop subdivision rules, which progressively moves edges towards a curve-like shape.

Load dae/cube.dae. Perform several iterations of loop subdivision on the cube. Notice that the cube becomes slightly asymmetric after repeated subdivisions. Can you pre-process the cube with edge flips and splits so that the cube subdivides symmetrically? Document these effects and explain why they occur. Also explain how your pre-processing helps alleviate the effects.
Yes, pre-processing the cube at specific points would allow for the cube to maintain its shape. The asymmetry is caused by the flipping occurring during the subdivision algorithm, which does not need to preserve symmetry and can cause certain regions to trend in a certain direction due to sharp corners. By locking in certain corners with pre-processing, the regions themselves will also be locked and will maintain their symmetry through subdivisions. This is because the divided edges along the corners will lead to the algorithm altering the corners less.
