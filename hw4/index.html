Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?
Changing the spring constant ks is equivalent to changing the force applied on each point mass per string. A low ks results in a cloth that retains its original form less, similar to a sheet of silk. A high ks results in a sturdier cloth with the opposite effect.
What about for density?
Changing the density of each point mass is equivalent to changing the gravity force applied on each point mass, since the increase in density is proportional to an increase in mass. This results in the cloth being pulled downwards more with denser masses, and pulled less with lighter masses.
What about for damping?
Changing the damping factor is equivalent to changing how energy flows out of the system per time step, and a lower damping factor results in the cloth to jitter more as it loses less energy per step and oscillates as it reaches equilibrium. A higher damping factor results in smoother movement throughout as the high damping factor removes any kind of oscillating behavior within.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Describe your implementation of handling collisions with spheres and planes.
Both collision cases have a similar approach. First, check if the point mass intersects the object of interest. For spheres, the distance between the point mass and the center of the sphere is less than the sphere’s radius. For planes, the distance between a point mass and a tangent point on the plane changes signs between time steps. When a collision occurs, calculate a correction vector to move the point mass back outside of the surface from where it originated and apply it to the point masses’ new position to simulate the collision occurring.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Describe your implementation of handling self-collisions.
A cloth object can have a large amount of point masses, so optimization for handling self-collisions is required for a reasonable simulation frame rate. To do this, I hashed every point-mass using its current position into a map of 3d boxes, effectively organizing the point masses into uniform bounding boxes. Handling self-collisions afterwards involves iterating through every point mass in the cloth and checking collisions with other point masses ONLY in the same 3D box by using the constructed hash map. The self-collision scheme uses a correction vector to offset any colliding point masses by some thickness.

With self-collisions, density and ks affect the final state of the cloth once gravity forces the cloth onto the plane. Higher density results in a more-crumpled cloth while a lower density results in a flatter cloth as each point mass resists clumping forces less and less. A higher ks results in a sheet-like clump as the spring forces on each point mass pull the cloth taut into a non-flat, yet sheet-like shape.

Part 5: Shaders

Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.
A shader program alters the look of a rendered environment by mapping raw pixel inputs from a GPU into a new color that is displayed on screen. Vertex and fragment shaders work in tandem to one another, as data from a vertex shader can be fed directly into a fragment shader for it to process. First, vertex shaders apply transforms to existing vertices before rasterizing the model itself, where fragment shaders alter pixel values on the camera space before it gets rendered.

The Blinn-Phong shading model builds upon the diffuse shading model by incorporating both specular and ambient light in its rendering as well. Specular, diffuse, and ambient lighting are summed together and scaled to some coefficients to depict realistic lighting on an object.
