Task 5: "Pixel sampling" for texture mapping

Explain pixel sampling in your own words and describe how you implemented it to perform texture mapping. Briefly discuss the two different pixel sampling methods, nearest and bilinear.
Pixel sampling is the act of grabbing data from points on an image, which are represented by position as pixels. This is used in the process of texture mapping, where pixels from a texture are sampled onto a triangle using barycentric coordinates to cover a triangle with a texture properly.
Nearest sampling takes a pixel sample from the pixel closest to the sampled coordinate in the texture, while bilinear takes the four nearest pixel samples according to the sampled coordinate and interpolates a final sample using the four pixel samples.

Check out the svg files in the svg/texmap/ directory. Use the pixel inspector to find a good example of where bilinear sampling clearly defeats nearest sampling. Show and compare four png screenshots using nearest sampling at 1 sample per pixel, nearest sampling at 16 samples per pixel, bilinear sampling at 1 sample per pixel, and bilinear sampling at 16 samples per pixel.
Comment on the relative differences. Discuss when there will be a large difference between the two methods and why.
Nearest sampling contains sharper colors and details but these sharper colors contrast with one another and may cause pixels to become more apparent. Bilinear appears more blurry due to the linear interpolation in the sampling stage, but there is a less apparent pixelated look to it upon closer inspection. A large difference between the two will occur when textures are stylized to look pixelated such as within video games, to which the nearest sampling method will produce a sharper look compared to bilinear.

Task 6: "Level Sampling" with mipmaps for texture mapping

Explain level sampling in your own words and describe how you implemented it for texture mapping.
Level sampling uses mipmapping to keep the image clear when the texture is sampled too frequently (i.e. the image is rendered smaller than its texture’s resolution). This has smaller, “pre-blurred,” versions of the texture that prevent aliasing when zoomed out. For our implementation, we calculate the mipmap level with UV coordinate approximation with nearby samples, and choose an appropriate mipmap level that prevents both too much as well as too little detail from interfering with the image quality. We implemented a couple methods of level sampling, those being nearest as well as linear interpolation, which in combination with bilinear sampling, creates trilinear sampling.

You can now adjust your sampling technique by selecting pixel sampling, level sampling, or the number of samples per pixel. Describe the tradeoffs between speed, memory usage, and antialiasing power between the three various techniques.
Increasing the number of samples per pixel (supersampling) is theoretically correct, increasing the accuracy of each pixel. However, increasing the sampling rate exponentially increases computation speed and memory usage, and relies on a very large number of samples to stop aliasing in very high frequency textures.
Pixel sampling helps with the image when it’s zoomed in by interpolating between pixels. Bilinear sampling as opposed to nearest sampling helps prevent hard pixel edges in the texture, while not having too much computational overhead since linear interpolation is relatively cheap.
Level sampling helps with sampling the image when it’s too small. Mipmapping creates logarithmically smaller versions of the image, which does require a bit more memory. However, as opposed to massive amounts of supersampling, it is much more efficient at preventing aliasing in shrunken textures.
